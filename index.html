A Closer Look Three apps I built to solve real daily problems, explained just for you, Wholee #2. Part 1 — The Meal Log App Where it started, and what it replaced THE PROBLEM When I was on spironolactone, a potassium-sparing diuretic, my doctor told me to limit potassium but gave me no specifics. So I had AI build me formulas that actually quantified what that meant. What it came out to was counterintuitive. I needed to consume more salt than normal and less potassium, and the ratio between the two had to stay within a heart-healthy range. The tricky part is that every meal shifts that ratio. An avocado is high in potassium, so if I ate one I'd need to increase my salt that day to bring the ratio back into range. The same thing applied to protein and calcium since those amounts varied meal to meal. THE BINDER SYSTEM So I made one page for each meal I ate regularly. Each page showed exactly how much extra salt, potassium substitute, protein powder, calcium, or magnesium to add that day depending on what was already in the meal. It also showed a grade for my sodium-to-potassium ratio so I could see at a glance whether I was in range. ￼ The grade section showing whether my sodium-to-potassium ratio was in range for that day The reason the pages had so many options listed was because when I was on spironolactone, it was actually harder than you'd think to get in enough sodium to counteract what the medication was doing. So options 1, 2, and 3 were different ways I could hit that sodium target depending on what I was eating that day. ￼ One page showing all the possible combinations for a single meal Each meal also had certain things that stayed the same every single time I ate it. One of those was eggs. I recently learned that choline deficiency is actually one of the bigger risk factors for dementia, and that eggs are essentially the only practical way to get enough of it. Without any eggs at all I'd be hitting maybe half the recommended daily amount, and most of that was already coming from the large amount of vegetables I eat. So I had to make it up. What I found is that on most days, adding just one egg gets me to around 80 to 90 percent of the RDA, though some days require two. So the number of eggs needed for that particular meal was always on the page. The pages also tracked how many extra magnesium supplements to take with each meal. I used to always need an extra one or two depending on the meal. Since I switched my vegetables from arugula and romaine to spinach and swiss chard though, I no longer need any additional magnesium at all. I also use a nutrition tracking app called Cronometer to log everything I eat. It shows exact intake for calories, protein, sodium, potassium, calcium, and dozens of other nutrients. I built a separate calculator that would take my current protein and calcium totals from Cronometer and tell me exactly how much yogurt, whey protein, milk, or some combination of all three I'd need to hit my targets without going over calories. That's why each meal's page and each day's plan included the specific gram amounts for milk, yogurt, or protein powder depending on what was needed that day. THE ROTATION GRID When I ran out of spironolactone and couldn't get it refilled due to not having insurance at the time, everything flipped overnight. Without the medication I can barely add extra salt without needing extra potassium to compensate. Same concept, opposite direction. So I still needed the pages. One thing I'm glad about is that I still have all those numbers in place, so if I ever get back on it someday I can see at a glance exactly what I should eat without having to recalculate everything from scratch. To get variety and work through my freezer, I made a 12-week meal rotation grid. It follows general nutrition recommendations, so beef once a week, chicken three times a week, seafood once or twice. I also added one meal a week of my own choice that didn't follow the guidelines, usually something like pork, pizza, or a quesadilla. I'll be adding corned beef hash to that rotation now too. Part of the reason for the grid was also to eliminate food waste. Before I had any kind of system, I'd end up with things expiring or getting forgotten in the freezer because there was no plan for when I was going to eat them. The grid gave each meal a designated slot so nothing got overlooked. Next to each meal I'd write in the Cronometer date for that entry plus squares where I could fill in the quantities of the extra items like milk, yogurt, whey, how many eggs for that day, and how many extra magnesium supplements. But as time went on I found myself needing to change those items or add more, and doing that would have meant reprinting the entire 12-week system. On top of that, I couldn't have more than 6 columns to the right or everything would start shifting off the page, and I couldn't decrease the height of the rows either because they had to be large enough that if I needed to erase something I wouldn't accidentally erase something nearby. ￼ The 12-week rotation grid — my attempt to simplify the binder pages system THE MEAL LOG APP The binder pages were always kept in the kitchen, so finding what I needed wasn't the issue. Moving everything into an app on my computer was the first step toward making the whole thing easier and more efficient. But if I didn't need to make any changes to a meal and just wanted to know the quantities of the extras, like how many eggs to have that day or what I needed to pull from the freezer to thaw, unplugging my laptop and carrying it into the kitchen felt like overkill. I wanted a way to get it onto my phone instead, which turned out to be a lot harder than I ever imagined. I just figured it was all stored on Google and would sync automatically, but that wasn't the case at all since none of this could be produced in Google to begin with. So I built the app. ￼ The main screen showing all five meals currently in the app Each meal has its own color-coded card. When you open one, you see the full recipe right there without having to look for it anywhere else. ￼ Chicken noodle soup opened up showing the recipe, photo, and notes section Below the recipe is the notes section. This is where I keep the Cronometer date for that meal along with all the extra quantities that change every time, like avocado weight, mandarin weights, whether I want kefir or not, and anything else I might need to add or remove. When I want to eat that meal again, I just copy that whole logged day in Cronometer into a new date and only have to tweak the few things that are different. Those notes stay there permanently so everything I need for that meal is always right there waiting for me. ￼ Typing an ingredient in the search bar pulls up every meal that contains it The app is pretty new so I've only entered 5 meals into it so far, but the idea is that every meal I eat regularly will eventually have its own card. This replaced the entire binder system. Part 2 — The Freezer Inventory App No more guessing what's left THE PROBLEM I have a drawer-style freezer, which means everything gets stacked on top of everything else. Trying to find a specific bag of food to pull out and thaw, or even just finding my portions for the next day, was taking me anywhere from 10 to 15 minutes every single time because I had to pull everything out one by one to find what I was looking for. ￼ ￼ Everything stacked on top of everything else in the drawer HOW I USED TO TRACK IT Before the app, I tracked everything on a spreadsheet that I printed out and put into plastic sheet protectors. I'd write the remaining quantity in each box using a permanent marker, and every time I removed something I'd erase it with a cotton pad soaked in rubbing alcohol and write in the new number. The squares had to be big enough so that when I removed the ink I wouldn't accidentally erase the numbers right next to it. If I needed to add or remove a food or change a bin location, I had to go back into the spreadsheet, re-sort everything, and reprint. ￼ The printed spreadsheet with handwritten quantities filled into each box THE APP So I built the Freezer Inventory app. Every item in my freezer is listed here organized by category, and each one shows which bin it's in, the current quantity, and the unit it's measured in. ￼ The main screen showing all items organized by category with bin locations and quantities YOUR QUESTION — HOW DO YOU UPDATE THE LOCATION? That bin column next to each item is exactly how I do it. My freezer has three sections, Top, Left, and Right. When I move something to a different spot I just click that dropdown and pick the new location. It saves instantly, no extra steps. ￼ One click on the dropdown to change an item's location ￼ The Meal Bag location button showing which bin the week's meals are currently stored in YOUR QUESTION — DO YOU HAVE TO UPDATE EACH ITEM INDIVIDUALLY? It depends on what needs updating, so let me break it down. For the Freezer Inventory, one example of a typical day would be that I eat one portion of frozen chicken, one portion of rice, one frozen cube of heavy cream, 100 grams of blueberries, one day's worth of vegetables, 100g of frozen milk, and 100g of frozen oat milk. Since the veggies and the blueberries are already on the due date system, that date is already set and I don't have to make any updates to those. For the rest I just click the minus button or double-click the line and type in the number that's left, which right now comes out to 6 items per day. The reason I'm currently tracking the milks is that I messed up a tea and coffee recipe I have that now requires me to use them up in a specific way, so I need to keep track until they run out. Once they do, I won't need to track them anymore, which means I'd typically only be updating about 3 items per day. It's also worth noting that a lot of items appear in both the Freezer Inventory and the Shopping Tracker. The blueberries are a good example. I don't track the quantity of bags in the Freezer Inventory because that's already being handled with a due date over in the Shopping Tracker. The reason those items show up in the Freezer Inventory at all is just so I know which bin to find them in. For the Shopping Tracker, most items sit there with a due date already set and don't need any updating until I buy more. I'll explain that system in more detail in Part 3. THE MEAL BAG With or without any system, I still have to physically dig through the drawer to find what I need and pull it out to thaw. But I figured I could save about an hour a week by pulling out a whole week's worth at once, which is also why meal planning matters so much. If I just decided on the day of what I wanted to eat, it wouldn't be thawed in time, and I'd spend 10 to 15 minutes every single day pulling bags out since the items for any one meal can be spread across two or three different bins. Pulling everything out for the whole week at once takes about 30 minutes, but 30 minutes once a week beats 10 to 15 minutes every single day, which adds up to around an hour and a half per week. So the savings come out to roughly an hour a week just by planning ahead. When I pull out a week's worth, all those frozen portions go into one bag together and that bag gets stored in whichever bin has more room at the time. That's what the Meal Bag location button at the top is for. It shows me which bin my meal bag is currently sitting in so I know exactly where to reach. What the Meal Bag section in the app solves is the counting confusion that used to come after all that. Before, I'd pull out say 3 servings of chicken and mark down that I had 8 left. But then later I'd second-guess myself. Did I already account for those 3 I pulled out, or not? Now when I move something into the Meal Bag it automatically deducts from my main inventory count right then. I can add items to the Meal Bag by clicking the Meal button next to any item in the main list, or by using the dropdown at the bottom of the Meal Bag section which shows every item in my inventory along with how much is left. ￼ Each item in the main list has a Meal button to move it into the Meal Bag ￼ The dropdown at the bottom of the Meal Bag shows every item with current quantities to choose from Once items are in the Meal Bag, each one has controls for tracking what happens to it. The minus button means I ate one serving. The plus button puts one serving back into the main inventory. The X cancels the item entirely and puts all of it back into the main inventory. And the trash icon marks something as done or eaten and clears it without affecting the main inventory count, for times when I already accounted for it separately. ￼ Each item in the Meal Bag has controls for eating, putting back, canceling, or clearing At the end of the week if I have anything left that I decided not to eat, I just hit the red Clear All button and everything goes back into the main inventory counts automatically. THE CHICKEN CALCULATOR One small but useful thing built into the Raw Chicken Breast entry is a calculator. Since chicken loses weight when it cooks, I type in the raw weight in grams and it instantly tells me how many cooked 4-oz servings that comes out to. So if I weigh the chicken before I cook it I already know exactly how many servings I'll end up with before I even start. ￼ Type in the raw weight and it instantly calculates how many cooked servings you'll get THE VEGETABLE SITUATION I freeze 16 days worth of vegetables at a time and can only fit 16 days worth in the freezer at once. I prepare them 4 days at a time and have to time it so I never run out. The problem was that if I skipped eating vegetables on any given day, it would throw off all my counts and I'd have to recount everything to figure out how many days were actually left. That's where the Shift Veggies button comes in, but that's part of the Shopping Tracker, so I'll cover it in Part 3. Part 3 — The Shopping Tracker App No more guessing what to buy, where to buy it, or when to buy it THE PROBLEM Before this app, I had no real system for grocery shopping. I'd go to one or two stores and just buy whatever looked good. I'd end up duplicating items, or not having enough of something when I wanted to make a specific meal, or having food go bad before I could use it. I also wasn't saving as much money as I could have been. When multiple stores carry the same item within a 50 cent to a dollar difference, that adds up fast. Just by buying each item at whichever store sells it cheapest, I was saving around $20 a week. HOW I USED TO TRACK IT I had a spreadsheet with all the same information I have now, every item, the store it came from, the price, notes on the best deals, and a due date for when I'd run out. But every time I needed to go shopping, just preparing for that trip took about 30 minutes. First I'd go through and mark an X next to everything I needed. Then I'd look up all the due dates to figure out which items could wait for a future trip. Then I'd update the formula in the Cash Need cell to only calculate what I was actually buying that day. Then I'd sort everything by store number so I could shop in order. And then I'd print the whole thing out and carry it with me to the store. Every single time. On top of all that, any time I needed to figure out a due date, I had to separately look up what the actual calendar date would be 13 days from now or however many days it was, because the spreadsheet didn't calculate that automatically. ￼ My old spreadsheet system THE APP Both systems have the same 301 items across 11 stores with prices, store numbers, notes, and due dates. The difference is that the app handles everything that used to take me 30 minutes to do manually. The due dates calculate automatically so I never have to look up what date is 13 days from today. Everything stays sorted the way I need it without me touching anything. I never have to print anything out. And I can check it from my phone in the kitchen or at the store without carrying a single piece of paper. ￼ The main screen showing items with checkmarks, due dates, store numbers, prices and notes THE DUE DATE SYSTEM Most items have a due date already set, which means they sit there quietly until it's time to buy more. I don't have to touch them at all until that date approaches. When I do buy more, I just update the quantity and the due date shifts forward automatically based on how many days worth I bought. For items I don't use every day, like avocados, I enter the number of days worth when I buy them and the date calculates from there. If I need to nudge a date forward or back without buying anything new, I click on the date and a small popup appears where I can either shift it by a number of days or set a specific new date directly. ￼ Clicking any due date opens this popup to shift it forward or back, or set a new date directly THE SHIFT VEGGIES BUTTON Since I freeze 16 days worth of vegetables at a time, if I skip a day of vegetables my due dates for all those items get thrown off. Before, I'd have to recount everything and manually figure out new dates for each veggie item. Now I just click the Shift Veggies button, type in how many days to push forward or pull back, and every single veggie item updates at once. ￼ One number and one click updates the due dates for every vegetable item at once SHOP MODE When it's time to actually go shopping, I click Shop Mode. The whole app switches to a clean easy-to-read view grouped by store number. Each item shows the price and any notes, and I just check it off as I go. Store 5 is H Mart, store 6 is Grocery Outlet, store 7 is Smart and Final, and so on, so I work through them in order without backtracking. ￼ Shop Mode groups everything by store in order, with large text and a checkoff box for each item YOUR QUESTION — HOW DO YOU UPDATE THE PRICES? If a price changes, which doesn't happen too often, I just click Edit on that item and type in the new price. The notes section is where I keep the price comparisons across different stores so I always know if I'm getting the best deal before I even leave the house. YOUR QUESTION — DO YOU HAVE TO UPDATE EACH ITEM INDIVIDUALLY? For the most part, no. The items with due dates sit there on their own until I buy more. The ones I check off for a shopping trip I just tap a checkmark next to. And the Shift Veggies button updates all the vegetable items at once with one entry. On a typical non-shopping day I don't have to touch the shopping app at all.
